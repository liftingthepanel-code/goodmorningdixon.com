Chapter One · Before Everything

The Wintun–Patwin People
Were Here First.

The first inhabitants of the Dixon area were the Wintun, known as the Patwin — a name derived from their own word meaning "native people." Their territory extended 90 miles from north to south and 40 miles from east to west, encompassing the Sacramento River valley and surrounding hills from Princeton in the north to the San Pablo and Suisun Bays in the south — the precise land on which every city in this corridor now stands.

The Wintun were, by every historical account, a peaceful people. They built their villages from willow branches covered in dried tule reeds. They fished the abundant waterways, gathered acorns from groves they tended carefully across generations, and wove baskets of extraordinary craft. Their settlements numbered more than 50 across this territory. They had lived in stewardship with this valley for thousands of years before any European set foot in California.

"The Yocha Dehe Wintun Nation — whose ancestors were the Patwin people of this region — continues its mission of language revitalization, cultural preservation, and protection of sacred sites across Yolo, Solano, Colusa, and Lake Counties today. Their story is the first chapter of Dixon's story. It will always be told here first."

Good Morning Dixon will not treat the Patwin history as a prologue to be cleared away before the "real" story begins. This gazette is in the process of reaching out directly to the Yocha Dehe Wintun Nation to ensure that what is written here is accurate, respectful, and worthy of the history it attempts to honor.

The Yocha Dehe Wintun Nation Today
The Federally Recognized Tribal Nation of the Patwin People

The Yocha Dehe Wintun Nation is headquartered in Brooks, CA — approximately 25 miles from Dixon. Their mission encompasses the revitalization of the Patwin language, the protection of sacred sites, and the preservation of a culture that has endured for thousands of years on this land. Three federally recognized Patwin tribes exist in this region. Their continued presence is not history — it is the living present. Any honest account of this valley begins and ends with acknowledging them.

Chapter Two · 1852–1878

The City That Packed Up and Moved Itself to the Future.

The European settlement that would become Dixon began as Silveyville, founded in 1852 by Elijah Silvey as a stopping point for miners traveling between the Pacific coast and Sacramento's gold fields. For nearly two decades, Silveyville thrived on that trade — until the railroad arrived and changed everything.

In 1868, the Central Pacific Railroad laid its tracks through Solano County and missed Silveyville by several miles. Dixon's founding generation did not complain. They did not petition. They moved. Buildings were relocated. Streets were laid. One of the first structures from that 1871 move — the Dixon Methodist Church at 209 N. Jefferson Street — still stands in Dixon today.

"Thomas Dickson donated 10 acres of his land for a railroad depot. The settlement was named Dicksville in his honor — until the first shipment from San Francisco arrived addressed simply to 'Dixon.' After two years of informal use, Dickson himself endorsed the simpler name."

On March 30, 1878, Dixon was formally incorporated as a city through a special act of the California Legislature, with an initial population of approximately 300 residents — one of the oldest incorporated cities in Solano County, built from the very first moment on the conviction that you do not wait for opportunity. You go to meet it.

The Agricultural Capital

From Gold Rush Waystation to California City.

Dixon grew quickly into one of Northern California's premier agricultural centers, shipping grain, wool, and livestock to Bay Area markets. The fertile Solano County soil produced wheat, barley, and the diverse crops that define Dixon's identity today.

Dixon earned its title as the Lamb Capital of the World through generations of sheep ranching that made Solano County famous across American agricultural markets. That identity — practical, hardworking, deeply rooted in the land — remains the foundation of everything this city is and everything it can become.

Chapter Three · A Century of Making History

148 Years of Building
Something Worth Knowing.

1875
California's Oldest District Fair

The Dixon May Fair began as a May Day picnic celebration in 1875 and has run continuously ever since — California's oldest district fair and home to the longest-running state-affiliated parade in the state. Every May, Dixon becomes the center of this region. A stage on the fairgrounds bears the name of Jon Pardi — the country star who grew up in Dixon and whose music has reached every corner of America. He hasn't forgotten where he came from.

1944
Dixon Broadcast to the World

NBC built a Voice of America shortwave transmitter site in Dixon during World War II — broadcasting under call signs KNBA, KNBH, KNBI, KNBC, and KNBX to allied nations across the Pacific. Dixon's signal reached Japan and the Philippines during the defining hour of the 20th century. There is also a Dixon Naval Radio Transmitter Facility still active today. This city was America's voice to the world at its most critical moment. That is not a footnote. It is a fact of history.

2014
A Guinness World Record

Dixon residents Matt and Mark Cooley, owners of Cool Patch Pumpkins, hold the Guinness World Record for the world's largest corn maze — 60 acres, set in 2014 after they broke their own prior record of 53 acres in 2012. The same creative drive that moved a city to a railroad in 1868 is still here, still building, still breaking records. The Jackson Fay Brown House and the Dixon Carnegie Library are on the National Register of Historic Places.

Chapter Four · The Great Injustice · Est. 2007

Dixon Built a Train Station.
Not One Train Has Ever Stopped.

This is not hyperbole. This is a documented, verifiable, nineteen-year failure of political will — and it is the single most clarifying fact about Dixon's relationship with the institutions that are supposed to serve it.

0
Scheduled Capitol Corridor
stops in Dixon since 2007
The city that moved itself to a railroad in 1868 is being passed over by that railroad in 2026.

In 2007, the City of Dixon completed construction of a replica of the original Dixon train station — built with State redevelopment money directly on the Capitol Corridor line, with a clear stated intent: to become an active stop on Capitol Corridor service between Sacramento and the Bay Area.

That was 2007. Today, 15 Capitol Corridor round trips per weekday pass through Dixon — through the heart of this city, past that station, past those empty platforms — continuing to Davis and Fairfield–Vacaville without stopping.

Dixon residents who need the Capitol Corridor must drive to another city to board it. They add miles, time, and cost to a commute that station was built to eliminate. A Dixon stop is officially proposed in the Capitol Corridor Vision Plan. The infrastructure exists. The case is made. What is missing is the political will to demand it.

Chapter Five · What Comes Next

The ingredients for something
extraordinary are already here.

01
🚂
Activate the Station

A Capitol Corridor stop transforms Dixon's entire story. Bay Area commuters. Remote workers. Sacramento professionals. Dixon becomes connectable and desirable. One infrastructure win this city has already paid for and waited nineteen years to receive.

02
🌾
Own the Agricultural Legacy

The Lamb Capital of the World. California's oldest fair. A 60-acre world record. Dixon's agricultural identity is a competitive advantage in an era that is rediscovering local food, authentic place, and rural character. No city in this corridor has what Dixon has.

03
🏛️
Build a Regional Voice

Dixon, Winters, Woodland, Davis. Four cities sharing a corridor, a climate, and a future. The vision that unites them under common infrastructure and shared leadership would create one of California's most compelling regional stories over the next generation.

04
🎡
Claim the Cultural Capital

California's oldest district fair. Two National Register of Historic Places properties. Jon Pardi's hometown. A Guinness World Record. Dixon has more cultural gravity than it claims. The right leadership makes this a destination — not a place people drive past on I-80.

05
📡
Amplify the Signal

Dixon once broadcast America's voice across the Pacific. That history is a metaphor worth keeping: this city has always had something worth saying. It needs the platform — and the leadership — to say it loudly enough that Sacramento cannot ignore it.

06
🔥
Remember Who We Are

Dixon's founders moved a city to meet opportunity. That is the standard every generation must meet. The city that waited nineteen years for a train to stop is not the city that moved itself in 1868. It is time to remember who Dixon is — and act accordingly.
