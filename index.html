Chapter One · Before Everything

The Wintun–Patwin People
Were Here First.

The first inhabitants of the Dixon area were the Wintun, known as the Patwin — a name given to them by American ethnographer Stephen Powers in 1877, derived from their own word meaning "native people." Their territory extended 90 miles from north to south and 40 miles from east to west, encompassing the Sacramento River valley and the surrounding hills from Princeton in the north to the San Pablo and Suisun Bays in the south — including the land on which every city in this corridor now stands.

The Wintun were, by every historical account, a peaceful people. They built their villages from willow branches covered in dried tule reeds, fished the abundant waterways, gathered acorns from groves they tended carefully over generations, and wove baskets of extraordinary craft from the tule reeds of the Sacramento Delta. They had lived in stewardship with this valley for thousands of years before any European set foot in California.

"The Wintun (Patwin) lived in stewardship with the land for the benefit of humans and the habitat. Their settlements numbered more than 50 across this territory. Their language, their culture, and their connection to this land are living things — not history."

Good Morning Dixon will not treat this as a prologue to be cleared away before the "real" history begins. The Yocha Dehe Wintun Nation — whose ancestors were the Patwin people of this region — continues its mission of language revitalization, cultural preservation, and protection of sacred sites in this very valley today. Their story is the first chapter of Dixon's story. It will always be told here first.

The Yocha Dehe Wintun Nation
The Federally Recognized Tribal Nation of the Patwin People

The Yocha Dehe Wintun Nation is headquartered in Brooks, CA — approximately 25 miles from Dixon. Their mission is the revitalization of Patwin language, the protection of sacred sites across Yolo, Solano, Colusa, and Lake Counties, and the preservation of a culture that has endured for thousands of years on this land. Three federally recognized Patwin tribes exist in this region. Their history belongs in any honest account of this valley.

Chapter Two · 1852–1878

The City That Packed Up and Moved Itself to the Future.

The European settlement that would become Dixon began not in its present location but in a community called Silveyville, founded in 1852 by Elijah Silvey as a stopping point for miners traveling between the Pacific coast and Sacramento's gold fields. For nearly two decades, Silveyville thrived on that trade — until the railroad arrived and changed everything.

In 1868, the Central Pacific Railroad laid its tracks through Solano County — and missed Silveyville by several miles. The response of Dixon's founding generation was not to complain, not to petition, not to wait. They decided to physically move their community to be closer to the tracks. Buildings were relocated. Streets were laid. One of the first structures still standing in Dixon from that 1871 move is the Dixon Methodist Church at 209 N. Jefferson Street.

"Thomas Dickson donated 10 acres of his land for the construction of a railroad depot. The settlement was named Dicksville in his honor — until the first rail shipment arrived from San Francisco mistakenly addressed to 'Dixon.' After two years of informal use, Dickson himself endorsed the simpler name."

On March 30, 1878, Dixon was formally incorporated as a city through a special act of the California Legislature, with an initial population of approximately 300 residents. It became one of the oldest incorporated cities in Solano County — built from the very beginning on the conviction that you do not wait for opportunity to find you. You go to meet it.

The Railroad Town

From Gold Rush Waystation to California City.

From its first act — an entire community relocating itself to meet a railroad — Dixon established the character of everything that followed. The city grew rapidly into one of Northern California's premier agricultural centers, shipping grain, wool, and livestock to Bay Area markets. The fertile Solano County soil produced wheat, barley, and later the diverse crops that define Dixon's agricultural identity today.

Dixon earned its title as the Lamb Capital of the World through generations of sheep ranching that made Solano County famous across American agricultural markets. That identity — practical, hardworking, rooted in the land — is still the foundation of everything this city is.

Chapter Three · A Century of Making History

148 Years of Building
Something Worth Knowing.

```
1875
California's Oldest Fair

The Dixon May Fair began as a May Day picnic celebration in 1875 and has run continuously ever since — making it California's oldest district fair and home to the longest-running state-affiliated parade in California. Every May, Dixon becomes the center of this region. A stage on the fairgrounds bears the name of Jon Pardi — the country star who grew up here and whose voice has reached every corner of America. Dixon made him. He hasn't forgotten it.

1944
Dixon Spoke to the World

NBC built a Voice of America shortwave transmitter site in Dixon during World War II — broadcasting under call signs KNBA, KNBH, KNBI, KNBC, and KNBX to allied nations across the Pacific. Dixon's signal reached Japan and the Philippines during the defining hour of the 20th century. There is also a Dixon Naval Radio Transmitter Facility still operating today. This is not a footnote in American military history. Dixon was America's voice to the world at its most critical moment.

2014
A World Record — Right Here

Dixon residents Matt and Mark Cooley, owners of Cool Patch Pumpkins, hold the Guinness World Record for the largest corn maze ever created — 60 acres of living agricultural artistry, set in 2014 after they broke their own prior record of 53 acres in 2012. The same creative drive that moved a city in 1868 is still producing world-class achievements. The Jackson Fay Brown House and the Dixon Carnegie Library are also on the National Register of Historic Places.
```

```
Chapter Four · The Great Injustice · Est. 2007

Dixon Built a Train Station.
Not One Train Has Stopped.

This is not a grievance. This is not an exaggeration. This is a documented, verifiable, nearly two-decade-long failure of political will — and it is the most clarifying single fact about Dixon's relationship with the institutions that are supposed to serve it.

0
Scheduled Capitol Corridor
stops in Dixon since 2007
The city that moved itself to a railroad in 1868 is being passed over by that railroad in 2026.

In 2007, the City of Dixon completed construction of a replica of the original Dixon train station — built with State redevelopment money on the Capitol Corridor line, with a clear and stated intent: to become an active stop on Capitol Corridor service between Sacramento and the Bay Area.

That was 2007. Today, 15 Capitol Corridor round trips per weekday pass directly through Dixon — through the center of this city, past that station, past those empty platforms — without stopping. The nearest stations are Davis to the east and Fairfield–Vacaville to the west.

Dixon residents who need the Capitol Corridor must drive to another city to board it. They add miles, time, and fuel cost to a commute the station was specifically built to eliminate. The train that Dixon's founding generation moved their entire city to meet is the same train that passes Dixon by, twice an hour, every single day.

A Dixon stop is officially proposed as a new infill station in the Capitol Corridor Vision Plan. The infrastructure exists. The route runs through the city. What is missing — the only thing that has ever been missing — is sustained, organized demand and the political will to deliver it.
```

Chapter Five · What Comes Next

The ingredients for something
extraordinary are already here.

01
🚂
Activate the Station

A Capitol Corridor stop changes Dixon's entire story. Bay Area commuters. Remote workers. Sacramento professionals. Dixon becomes connectable — accessible — desirable. One infrastructure win that Dixon has already paid for and been waiting 19 years to receive.

02
🌾
Own the Agricultural Legacy

The Lamb Capital of the World. California's oldest fair. A 60-acre world record. Dixon's agricultural identity is a competitive advantage in a 21st century economy that is rediscovering local food, authentic place, and rural character. No city in this corridor has what Dixon has.

03
🏛️
Build a Regional Voice

Dixon, Winters, Woodland, Davis — four cities sharing a corridor, a climate, and a future. The vision that unites them under common infrastructure and shared leadership would create one of California's most compelling regional stories over the next generation.

04
🎡
Claim the Cultural Capital

California's oldest district fair. Two National Register of Historic Places. Jon Pardi's hometown. A Guinness World Record. Dixon has more cultural gravity than it claims credit for. The right leadership makes this city a destination — not a place people drive past on I-80.

05
📡
Amplify the Signal

Dixon once broadcast America's voice across the Pacific. That history is a metaphor worth keeping: this city has always had something worth saying. It just needs the platform — and the leadership — to say it loudly enough that Sacramento has no choice but to listen.

06
🔥
Remember Who We Are

Dixon's founders moved a city to meet opportunity. That is the standard every generation must meet. The city that quietly waited 19 years for a train that won't stop is not the city that moved itself in 1868. It is time to remember who Dixon is — and act accordingly.
