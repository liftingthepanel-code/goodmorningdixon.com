Good Morning, Dixon.

There is a city in Northern California with a 148-year history most people don't know. A fair older than any in the state. A World War II broadcast station that reached the Pacific. This is that city. This is its story.

Chapter One

Before Dixon —
The Patwin People.

Long before any settler arrived, before any railroad was surveyed, before any deed was recorded — the Patwin people called this valley home. For thousands of years, their villages lined the waterways of what is now Solano and Yolo County. They fished the tule marshes, hunted the valley oak woodlands, and built a civilization that measured its continuity not in decades but in millennia.

The Patwin territory encompassed precisely the land on which Dixon, Winters, Woodland, and Davis now stand. Every acre of this valley carries their history in its soil. The waterways they named, the trails they carved, the land they stewarded — all of it forms the foundation on which everything else in this story was built.

"The Yocha Dehe Wintun Nation — whose ancestors were the Patwin — continues its mission of language revitalization, cultural preservation, and protection of sacred sites across this very region today. Their story is not a prologue to Dixon's history. It is the first chapter — and it will be told here with the dignity it deserves."

This gazette will not footnote this history. It will not relegate it to a single paragraph. The Patwin people were here first. That fact shapes everything that follows.

Chapter Two

The City That Moved Itself to Meet the Future.

In 1871, a California farming community named Silveyville made a decision that would define everything that followed. When the California Pacific Railroad laid its tracks through Solano County, the people of Silveyville didn't wait for the railroad to come to them. They picked up their settlement and moved it to meet the tracks.

A farmer named Thomas Dickson donated 10 acres of his land for a depot. The new settlement was called Dicksville. The first rail shipment arrived from San Francisco addressed simply to "Dixon" — and the cleaner name prevailed. On March 19, 1878, Dixon was formally incorporated as a city, one of the oldest in Solano County.

The city grew quickly. Grain. Wool. Livestock. The railroads carried Dixon's produce to Bay Area markets and returned with commerce, ambition, and settlers. Dixon became one of Northern California's premier agricultural centers. By the time the 20th century arrived, it had established itself as the Lamb Capital of the World — a title it still holds today.

"Dixon's founders didn't sit and wait for opportunity. They moved an entire community to meet it. That is not just history. It is a standard."

The Record

What Dixon Built.

From that first act of collective ambition — an entire community relocating itself to a railroad — everything else in Dixon's history follows. The fair. The broadcast station. The world record. The train station that was built and then abandoned by the very institution Dixon moved itself to meet.

This is a city that has earned better than it has received. The evidence is in every chapter that follows.

1885
California's Oldest Fair

The Dixon May Fair has run without interruption since 1885 — predating the Solano County Fair by 64 years. The oldest fair in the entire state of California. Every May, Dixon becomes the center of this region. A stage on the fairgrounds bears the name of Jon Pardi — Dixon's own country star, whose voice has reached every corner of America.

1944
Dixon Spoke to the World

NBC built a Voice of America shortwave transmitter in Dixon during World War II. Under call signs KNBA, KNBH, and KNBI, Dixon's signal broadcast to Japan and the Philippines at the defining hour of the 20th century. This was not a footnote in the war effort. Dixon was America's voice to the Pacific. That is a fact that deserves to be said plainly.

2014
A World Record — Right Here

Dixon residents Matt and Mark Cooley hold the Guinness World Record for the largest corn maze ever created — 60 acres at Cool Patch Pumpkins. The same ingenuity that moved a community in 1871, that broadcast to the Pacific in 1944, is still here. Still building. Still breaking records. Dixon doesn't just grow crops. It grows achievements.

Chapter Four — The Great Injustice

Dixon Built a Train Station.
The Train Won't Stop.

This is not hyperbole. This is not a complaint. This is a documented, verifiable, nineteen-year-old failure of political will — and it is the single most clarifying fact about Dixon's relationship with the institutions that are supposed to serve it.

0
Scheduled train stops in Dixon
since the station was completed in 2007

The city that moved itself to a railroad in 1871 is being passed over by that railroad in 2026.

In 2007, the City of Dixon completed construction of a full train station — a beautiful replica of the original 1871 depot — directly on the Capitol Corridor line between Sacramento and the Bay Area. State redevelopment funds paid for it. The intent, stated clearly and on record, was for it to become an active stop on California's Capitol Corridor service.

That was nineteen years ago. Today, up to fifteen Capitol Corridor round trips pass through Dixon every weekday — through the heart of this city, past that station, past those platforms — and continue without stopping to Davis and Fairfield-Vacaville.

Dixon residents who need rail access to Sacramento or the Bay Area must drive to another city first. They add miles, time, and cost to a commute that the train was specifically designed to eliminate. The station sits. The trains pass. The clock runs.

Chapter Five — What Comes Next

The ingredients for something
extraordinary are already here.

01
🚂
Activate the Station

A Capitol Corridor stop transforms Dixon's economic story entirely. Bay Area commuters. Remote workers. Sacramento professionals. Dixon becomes commutable — accessible — desirable. One infrastructure win changes the entire trajectory of this city's growth.

02
🌾
Own the Agricultural Identity

The Lamb Capital of the World. California's oldest fair. A 60-acre world record. Dixon's agricultural heritage is not nostalgia — it is a competitive advantage in a 21st century economy that is rediscovering the value of local food, rural community, and authentic place.

03
🏛️
Build a Regional Voice

Dixon, Winters, Woodland, Davis. Four cities. One corridor. One shared future. The vision that unites them — under common infrastructure, shared identity, and coordinated leadership — would create one of California's most compelling regional stories over the next generation.

04
🎡
Claim the Cultural Capital

The oldest fair in California. A Carnegie library on the National Register of Historic Places. Jon Pardi's hometown. A Guinness World Record. Dixon has more cultural gravity than it claims credit for. The right stewardship makes this city a destination — not a stopover.

05
📡
Amplify the Signal

Dixon once broadcast America's voice to the Pacific. That history is a metaphor worth keeping: this city has always had something worth saying. It just needs the platform — and the leadership — to say it loudly enough that Sacramento has no choice but to listen.

06
🔥
Remember Who You Are

The founders of this city moved it to meet the railroad. That is the standard by which every generation of Dixon leadership must be measured. The city that quietly waited nineteen years for a train that won't stop is not the city of 1871. It is time to remember who Dixon is.
