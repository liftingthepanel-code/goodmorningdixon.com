The Argument · Built from Facts

Four reasons this
has to change now.

01
The infrastructure already exists

Dixon completed its Capitol Corridor station in 2007 using State redevelopment funds — money specifically intended to create an active rail stop. The platforms are built. The tracks run directly through the city. There is no infrastructure argument against a Dixon stop. There is only political will, or the lack of it.

02
It is already in the plan

A Dixon stop is officially proposed in the Capitol Corridor Vision Plan — the long-range strategic document for the corridor's future. This is not a fringe demand. It is an officially recognized, documented proposal that has simply never been executed. The plan exists. The station exists. The trains run. The stop does not.

03
Dixon residents are being failed

Every Dixon resident who needs the Capitol Corridor must drive to another city — Davis or Fairfield–Vacaville — to board it. They add miles, time, and cost to a commute that a Dixon stop would eliminate. The station was built to solve this. Nineteen years later, the problem remains. That is not a planning failure. It is a leadership failure.

04
The region needs it

Dixon sits between Sacramento and the Bay Area on one of California's busiest rail corridors. A Dixon stop serves commuters across the Dixon–Davis–Woodland region, reduces highway congestion on I-80, connects agricultural communities to economic centers, and is exactly the kind of regional transit investment California claims to prioritize. The case writes itself.

Our History · Why This Matters More

Dixon has always
moved toward opportunity.

The European settlement that would become Dixon began as Silveyville, founded in 1852. When the Central Pacific Railroad laid its tracks through Solano County in 1868 and missed Silveyville by several miles, Dixon's founders did not complain. They did not petition. They moved the entire city to meet the railroad.

Buildings were relocated. Streets were relaid. One of the first structures from that 1871 move — the Dixon Methodist Church — still stands in Dixon today. On March 30, 1878, Dixon was formally incorporated, built from its very first moment on the conviction that you do not wait for opportunity. You go to meet it.

That same instinct built California's oldest district fair, which has run continuously since 1875. It produced a Guinness World Record corn maze. It sent Dixon's radio signal across the Pacific to allied nations during World War II. It raised Jon Pardi, who took Dixon's name around the world.

"A city that moved itself to a railroad in 1868 should not have to wait nineteen years for that railroad to stop. Dixon's founders would not recognize this patience. It is time to remember who we are."

The injustice of the train station is not just a transit issue. It is a statement about whether Dixon's history of bold, forward action is still alive in its leadership — or whether it has been replaced by the kind of quiet acceptance that Dixon was never built for.

The Wintun–Patwin People Were Here First
The First Inhabitants of This Valley

Before any of this history, the land on which Dixon stands was home to the Wintun, known as the Patwin — whose territory encompassed the full Sacramento River valley. The Yocha Dehe Wintun Nation, their federally recognized descendant tribe, is headquartered 25 miles from Dixon today. Their story is the first chapter of Dixon's story and will always be told here first.
